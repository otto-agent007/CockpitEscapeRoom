# Game design

## Premise

A DC-9 is safely parked in a hangar for a commemorative Father’s Day legacy flight. A fictional lockout protects the flight deck until the family crew demonstrates what it learned from an expert captain. The tone is warm, capable, and playful—not disaster-driven.

## Main DC-9 level

The final main level should contain five short puzzles. These are design targets, not real aircraft procedures.

### 1. Legacy power panel

Players inspect a small cluster of fictionalized switches and restore three circuits in a logical sequence. Crew Mode provides plain-language labels. Captain Mode uses compact abbreviations and panel clues. A wrong order resets only the current sequence.

Reward: the first instrument and a Memphis route strip illuminate.

### 2. Southern funnel route strip

Players choose or arrange several short feeder routes around MEM. Use airport codes such as LIT, JAN, BHM, BTR, SHV, MOB, STL, BNA, SDF, and IND as atmosphere. Crew Mode includes city names; Captain Mode relies more on geography and route-card context.

Avoid making exact airline statistics or dates required answers.

Reward: the route map and Concourse B activity come alive.

### 3. Connection-bank challenge

A small number of arriving passengers or bags must be matched to onward flights within a fictional connection bank. The puzzle expresses the rhythm of the Memphis hub without reproducing a real schedule.

Reward: a mechanical departure board flips and reveals a personal memory.

### 4. Captain’s log decoder

A logbook, family photo, saying, or career clue supplies a short code. This is the most personal puzzle and should use content the family will recognize.

Reward: the captain’s side of the cockpit gains full panel lighting.

### 5. Hangar release sequence

Players combine clues retained from earlier puzzles and complete a final switch or dial sequence. Completed progress cannot be lost, and the reveal must run only once.

Reward: hangar doors open and the Father’s Day message appears.

## Modes

### Crew Mode

- Clear labels and city names.
- Three progressive hint levels.
- More obvious hotspot treatment.
- Designed for mixed-age family play.

### Captain Mode

- Compact labels and fewer decorative prompts.
- More airport codes and authentic-looking route paperwork.
- Environmental DC-9/Memphis details.
- Same number of puzzles and no unfair trivia.
- Unlocks the red Model Y reward.

## Airbus bonus: Type Transition

After the DC-9 conclusion, a separate model-specific Airbus cockpit loads. The transition should celebrate adaptability from analog instrumentation to a modern glass flight deck.

The bonus contains one substantial challenge rather than five smaller puzzles. A good structure is to transfer clues gathered in the DC-9 into a simplified, fictional modern route display. The exact control vocabulary and cockpit geometry must wait until the aircraft model is confirmed.

## Red Model Y reward

Captain Mode opens the hangar to reveal a polished red Model Y. The current starter uses a red low-detail proxy only. Production choices require an original model or a license compatible with web distribution.

Suggested achievement copy:

**DC-9 Captain · Airbus Qualified · Electric Ground Crew**

## Mars Easter egg

A tiny red destination indicator in the Airbus cockpit unlocks a thirty-second Mars diversion. The Model Y becomes a humorous surface vehicle, and the captain receives the rank:

**Commander, Mars Transport Division**

The Easter egg remains optional and may never block the main ending.

## Difficulty and timing

Target total play time:

- Crew Mode main level: 15–25 minutes.
- Captain Mode main level: 20–30 minutes.
- Airbus bonus: 5–10 minutes.
- Mars Easter egg: under 2 minutes.

Playtest with family members who know little about aviation. If a clue requires external research, it is too hard for the main path.
